Foodborne illness is a persistent global health threat. They happen when harmful pathogens (like bacteria, viruses or parasites) get into the food we eat. In the United States alone, they cause over 48 million cases of illness, 128,000 hospitalizations, and 3,000 deaths every year, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC).

While foodborne diseases have existed throughout history, the recent outbreaks are hardly isolated incidents. Certain global conditions, both logistical and environmental, could be leading to more frequent and severe outbreak events.

According to Kristie L. Ebi, professor of Environmental and Occupational Health Sciences at the University of Washington, climate change adds another layer of complexity and could be a major driver of this public health crisis.

‚ÄúPathogens like E. coli and Salmonella thrive in warmer, wetter conditions,‚Äù explains Ebi. ‚ÄúAs global temperatures rise, we‚Äôre seeing shifts in how and where these pathogens proliferate, including longer seasons of contamination.‚Äù

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has also highlighted the increased risk of foodborne diseases as a direct consequence of climate change, predicting that the risk will likely increase in the coming decades without significant intervention being taken.

In response to these risks and outbreaks, food safety experts are urging an upgraded and multifaceted approach to prevention. ‚ÄúFrom farm to table, every step of the supply chain must adapt to the realities of a warming planet,‚Äù says Wiedmann. "Ensuring food safety in the face of climate change requires a comprehensive strategy that includes monitoring, regulation, and consumer education."
